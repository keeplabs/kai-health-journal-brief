Designer brief · Kai · v0.1

A journal for the in-between days.

Kai's second value proposition. A quiet, conversational memory of what it's actually like to live with a rare disease between doctor visits — written from the patient's own words, surfaced when they need it.

Brief at a glance

  • 01Feature — Health Journal, sitting alongside For You as Kai's second main destination.
  • 02Audience — patients managing chronic rare diseases, and the caregivers supporting them.
  • 03Outcome — better doctor visits, less mental load for caregivers, a record built passively from conversations and check-ins.
  • 04Out of scope — voice memos (coming later), care-team collaboration, integrations with EHR systems.
v0.1 · May 2026 For designers
The opportunity

The real story of living with a chronic condition happens between visits.

Doctor appointments are short. The disease isn't. The hardest parts — rough mornings, small wins, symptom flareups, the practical chaos of meds and meals — happen in the weeks in between, and by the time the next appointment rolls around, most of it is forgotten or compressed into a sentence.

For the patient

Living with this is the full-time job. The visit is the report.

When patients try to summarize months of lived experience in a 15-minute appointment, the texture gets lost. The "it interfered with the team call" moment becomes "itching has been bad." The week where evening doses before 9 PM clearly helped becomes a shrug.

A journal that writes itself from the conversations they're already having with Kai means they walk into appointments with the real picture — not a remembered one.

For the caregiver

It's all in my head, or on scraps of paper, or in a notes app I can't find.

Caregivers are doing invisible administrative work — tracking symptoms, meds, mood, appointments — while also showing up emotionally. That tracking burden is one of the most exhausting parts of caregiving, and the one that almost never gets named.

One place that's building the picture from check-ins and conversations lifts a real weight. Not by adding another thing to track, but by capturing what's already being said.

Acceptance criteria

The four capabilities the shipped feature must support.

Stated at the level of what a patient or caregiver can do — not at the level of screens or components.

01

Track and view their journal.

Patients can see what's been captured about their lived experience — symptoms, mood, medications, conversation moments — in one place inside Kai.

02

Generate summaries on demand, and save them.

At any time, the patient can ask Kai to summarize their journal — for any timeframe, any focus — and keep that summary so they can return to it later.

03

Share with the doctor.

A summary can be sent to the patient's doctor or care team. PDF is the working artifact for now. Future direction: a care-team view that the patient or caregiver grants access to — or a shareable link — letting the doctor see the live picture rather than a static snapshot. Design for the PDF flow now, but keep that future in peripheral vision.

04

Capture from structured and unstructured input.

Kai already does a version of this — if a patient asks for a check-in summary today, Kai pulls together themes across energy, itching, mood, sleep, and coping. The Health Journal extends that capability: not just check-in data, but anything said in conversation ("the itch was worst this morning") becomes part of the record, alongside the structured entries from onboarding and daily check-ins.

Design considerations

Not formal acceptance, but worth holding while you design. Each emerged from the prototype work and deserves a deliberate take — not a default.

  • i.Highlights vs full feed. The journal home likely needs both — a curated default and a "see all entries" path. Worth deciding what makes the highlights cut.
  • ii.Sparse-data fallback. Most summaries in the first weeks will be drawn from very little data. Charts will be meaningless. What does a useful summary look like when there's almost nothing structured to graph?
  • iii.Privacy at the share step. Sharing surfaces words the patient said in chat. They should be able to see what's about to leave Kai, and have it not feel like surveillance.
  • iv.Tone. Kai is a coach and a companion — summaries should sound like them, not like a medical chart. Avoid clinical or report-like voice in the output.
